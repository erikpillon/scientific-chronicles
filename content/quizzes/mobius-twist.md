intro: "Imagine taking a standard strip of paper, giving it a single half-twist, and taping the two ends together to create a classic Möbius strip."
question: "If you take a pair of scissors and cut this loop exactly down the center line all the way around, what do you end up with?"
options: 
  - "Two separate, smaller Möbius strips"
  - "Two interlocking loops of paper"
  - "One single, long loop with two full twists"
  - "The strip disintegrates into separate pieces"
correct_answer: 3
hint: "A Möbius strip famously has only one side and only one edge. When you slice down the middle, you aren't cutting it into two separate edges—you are traveling along its single, continuous boundary."
explanation: "The correct answer is **3: One single, long loop with two full twists**. Because a Möbius strip has only one continuous side and one continuous boundary, cutting it down the center line doesn't split it into two independent shapes. Instead, it unrolls the single edge into a single, longer closed loop that now possesses two full twists and is no longer non-orientable. If you were to cut *that* resulting loop down the center again, you would then get two beautifully interlocking loops!"
