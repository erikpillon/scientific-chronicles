Standard X-rays are great for a quick look, but they're basically shadows – flattening a 3D object onto a 2D plane. What if doctors needed to see *inside* with true depth? Enter the brilliant minds of Godfrey Hounsfield and Allan Cormack, who independently developed the technology behind computed tomography (CT) scans. Debuting for clinical use in 1972, CT scanners revolutionized medicine by taking multiple X-ray images from different angles around an object (like a patient's head) and using computers to reconstruct them into cross-sectional, 3D images. This groundbreaking invention earned them the Nobel Prize in Medicine in 1979 and completely changed diagnostics, allowing doctors to visualize organs, bones, and tissues in unprecedented detail, without ever lifting a scalpel. It truly brought a new dimension to medical insight!
