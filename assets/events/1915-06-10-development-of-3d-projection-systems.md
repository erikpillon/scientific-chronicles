Before you could watch a dinosaur roar out of the screen, someone had to figure out how to project two different images simultaneously, one for each eye. Early pioneers like William Friese-Greene patented stereoscopic projection concepts in the late 19th century, leading to experiments with devices that could create the illusion of depth. The earliest practical 3D projectors often used the anaglyph method (those red and blue glasses!) to filter two overlaid images. Later, in the 1950s, the development of polarized projection systems, using two synchronized projectors and special filters, brought a more comfortable and color-accurate 3D experience to the masses. These ingenious devices, which precisely align and project distinct left and right eye images, are the unsung heroes that make 3D cinema possible, tricking our brains into seeing depth where there's only a flat screen.
