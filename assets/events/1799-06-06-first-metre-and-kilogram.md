Before 1799, measuring things was an absolute nightmare. Every single town in Europe had a different definition for a foot or a pound. You could buy a pound of flour in one city and get ripped off in the next town over because their "pound" was completely different. 

The French Revolutionaries decided to fix this chaos. They defined a "metre" as exactly one ten-millionth of the distance from the North Pole to the Equator. To make sure nobody could argue about it, they literally cast a definitive bar out of solid platinum, cut to that exact length, and locked it in a secure archive in Paris. They did the same thing with a platinum cylinder for the kilogram. 

For nearly two centuries, if you wanted to know exactly how long a metre was, you had to compare it to that specific metal bar in France. It wasn't until the 1980s that scientists finally ditched the physical bar and redefined the metre using the speed of light. But that platinum bar is the reason your tape measure actually makes sense today. 📏🌍
