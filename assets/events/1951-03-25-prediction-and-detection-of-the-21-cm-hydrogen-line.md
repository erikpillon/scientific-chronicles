Imagine trying to map a huge, dark forest when all you have is a telescope. That's what astronomers faced with our galaxy, which is full of neutral hydrogen gas that doesn't emit visible light. Enter the brilliant idea from Dutch astronomer H.C. van de Hulst in 1944: hydrogen atoms, under very specific conditions, should emit a faint radio wave at a wavelength of 21 centimeters. Fast forward to 1951, when Harold Ewen and Edward Purcell at Harvard successfully detected this cosmic whisper! This wasn't just a cool observation; it was a game-changer. The 21-cm line allowed astronomers to pierce through the cosmic dust and map the spiral arms of our Milky Way and other galaxies, revealing their structure and dynamics in a way never before possible. It truly opened up the "dark" side of the universe for study!
