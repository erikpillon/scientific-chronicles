Before 3D movies and VR headsets, capturing the world in glorious three dimensions was a bit of a trick. While stereoscopic photography (using two cameras) dates back to the 19th century, achieving *color* 3D images that *didn't* require special viewers was a whole other beast. Enter Gabriel Lippmann, the French physicist who won a Nobel Prize for his method of color photography based on interference phenomena. Building on this, Lippmann also explored "integral photography" in 1908, a method for capturing a scene from multiple perspectives on a single photographic plate, which could then reproduce a full 3D image viewable from different angles without glasses. While technically challenging and not widely adopted for decades, Lippmann’s foresight laid crucial theoretical groundwork for autostereoscopic displays and lenticular printing we see today. He was truly seeing the world in a different dimension!
