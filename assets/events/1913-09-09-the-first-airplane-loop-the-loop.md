In the nascent days of aviation, simply staying airborne was considered a feat. But for daredevils like French aviator Adolphe Pégoud, merely flying wasn't enough. On September 9, 1913, Pégoud, already famous for being the first person to parachute from an airplane, decided to take things up a notch – literally. In a spectacular display over Buc, France, he executed the world's first sustained loop-the-loop in a Blériot Type XI monoplane. Spectators watched in disbelief as Pégoud's plane climbed, arced completely over, and then descended, returning to its original trajectory. This wasn't just a circus trick; it was a revelation. It demonstrated the incredible maneuverability of early aircraft and challenged conventional wisdom about flight dynamics, proving that planes could withstand forces previously thought impossible. Pégoud's audacious stunt not only captivated the public and ignited an interest in aerobatics but also opened the eyes of engineers and military strategists to the potential for aerial combat maneuvers, forever changing how we viewed the capabilities of aircraft.
