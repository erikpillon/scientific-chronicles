The early 1980s were a terrifying time, as a new, deadly disease known as AIDS spread globally. The race to identify its cause was intense. In 1983, a team at the Pasteur Institute in France, led by Luc Montagnier and Françoise Barré-Sinoussi, made a groundbreaking discovery: they isolated a retrovirus from a patient with lymphadenopathy, which they called Lymphadenopathy-Associated Virus (LAV). A year later, American scientist Robert Gallo’s lab independently identified a retrovirus, HTLV-III, as the cause of AIDS. Eventually, it was confirmed that LAV and HTLV-III were the same virus, now known as Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). This monumental identification was the critical first step in understanding the disease, developing diagnostic tests, and, eventually, creating life-saving antiretroviral therapies that have profoundly changed the course of the epidemic.
