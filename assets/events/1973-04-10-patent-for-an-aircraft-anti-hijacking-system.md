The 1960s and early 70s were wild times for air travel, with skyjackings becoming an alarming trend. Enter Bernard J. Lonergan, who, on September 8, 1971, filed for a patent that promised to turn the tables on aerial hijackers. Granted on April 10, 1973, Lonergan's "Aircraft Anti-Hijacking System" (US Patent 3,726,493) was a bold concept designed to prevent unauthorized control of an aircraft, effectively making the cockpit an impenetrable fortress against those with nefarious intentions. Imagine a system that could disable flight controls from within the cabin or even on the ground, or activate remote systems to land the plane safely away from the hijackers' influence. While the specific implementation evolved, this patent marked a crucial early step in the long, ongoing battle to ensure that a flight's destination remains firmly in the hands of the pilots and air traffic control, not unexpected intruders. It was a tangible response to a very real and terrifying threat, paving the way for the security measures we often take for granted today.
