Before atomic clocks and GPS, telling accurate time was a serious astronomical endeavor, and for much of the world, it all revolved around a single instrument: the Airy Transit Circle. Designed by the formidable Astronomer Royal, George Biddell Airy, and installed at the Royal Observatory Greenwich in 1850, this magnificent telescope and timing device wasn't just a fancy bit of kit. It was the linchpin for global navigation and timekeeping. By precisely observing the moment stars crossed the local meridian, the Airy Transit Circle determined Greenwich Mean Time (GMT), which was adopted as the prime meridian of the world in 1884. Imagine a device so accurate that it became the reference point for maps, sea charts, and railway timetables across an entire empire! It allowed ships to navigate accurately across vast oceans and synchronized the burgeoning railway networks, fundamentally shaping how we perceive and use time. This instrument wasn't just observing the heavens; it was anchoring the world in a precise, standardized understanding of "now."
