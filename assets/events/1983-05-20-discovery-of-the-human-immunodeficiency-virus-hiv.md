After the terrifying emergence of AIDS in the early 1980s, the race was on to find the culprit. In a medical detective story filled with high stakes and eventual controversy, scientists finally identified the 'AIDS virus.' In 1983, Luc Montagnier's team at the Pasteur Institute in France isolated a new retrovirus from a patient with lymphadenopathy, initially calling it LAV (Lymphadenopathy-Associated Virus). A year later, Robert Gallo's group at the National Cancer Institute in the U.S. confirmed this finding, rebranding it HTLV-III. It was eventually named Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV). This monumental discovery didn't just solve the medical mystery; it provided the crucial target for diagnostic tests and antiviral therapies, turning a death sentence into a manageable condition for many. It was a scientific breakthrough born from urgency, proving that even the most elusive pathogens can be unmasked by relentless research.
