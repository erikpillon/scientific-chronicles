For most of the 20th century, if you wanted a phone, you dealt with AT&T. The 'Bell System' was a massive, vertically integrated monopoly that controlled everything from local service to long-distance and equipment manufacturing. But by the late 1970s, the U.S. Justice Department decided this wasn't good for competition or innovation. After years of antitrust litigation, a settlement was reached, leading to the monumental divestiture on January 1, 1984. AT&T shed its 22 local operating companies, which were reorganized into seven independent Regional Bell Operating Companies (RBOCs), dubbed 'Baby Bells.' It was like a corporate earthquake, creating a competitive market that eventually paved the way for cell phones, the internet, and the diverse telecom landscape we know today. Monopolies, it seems, can sometimes be too big for their own good.
