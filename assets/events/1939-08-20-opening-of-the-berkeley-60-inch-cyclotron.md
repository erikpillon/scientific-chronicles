Ernest O. Lawrence was obsessed with smashing atoms, and he knew bigger was better. His quest led to the creation of the 60-inch cyclotron at the University of California, Berkeley, which became operational in 1939. This wasn't just a bigger version of his earlier models; it was a behemoth that could accelerate particles to unprecedented energies, enabling scientists to delve deeper into the secrets of the atomic nucleus. This cyclotron became a powerhouse for discoveries, leading to the creation of new synthetic elements, advancements in nuclear medicine, and a Nobel Prize for Lawrence. It was a tangible symbol of the power of big science and a testament to human ingenuity in wrestling secrets from the subatomic world.
