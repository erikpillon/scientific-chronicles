Before 1956, people called them 'thinking machines' or pondered 'cybernetics,' but a specific name for the pursuit of machine intelligence was missing. That's when computer scientist John McCarthy brilliantly coined the term 'Artificial Intelligence' and organized the legendary Dartmouth Summer Research Project. Kicking off in July 1956, this workshop brought together pioneering minds like Marvin Minsky and Claude Shannon. For ten weeks, these academic rebels brainstormed how to make machines simulate human intelligence, tackling everything from problem-solving to language. While it didn't solve AI overnight (surprise!), it crucially defined AI as a distinct academic field, energized research, and set the stage for all the smart algorithms, self-driving cars, and surprisingly eloquent chatbots we marvel at today. It was the Big Bang for AI as we know it.
