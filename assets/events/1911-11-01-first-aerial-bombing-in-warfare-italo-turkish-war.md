In the annals of warfare, November 1, 1911, marks a rather explosive milestone. During the Italo-Turkish War, an Italian aviator, Second Lieutenant Giulio Gavotti, took to the skies over Libya in a flimsy Etrich Taube monoplane. His mission? To drop four small bombs – literally by hand – over Turkish positions near Ain Zara and Tripoli. While these early 'bombs' were more like glorified grenades, weighing a mere 2 kg each, the psychological impact and the precedent set were immense. This wasn't just a casual toss; it was the dawn of aerial bombardment, demonstrating a terrifying new dimension in military strategy. No longer were armies safe from attacks from above, and the sky itself became a battlefield. This seemingly small act irrevocably altered the course of military history, paving the way for the massive aerial campaigns of the World Wars and beyond. Gavotti's crude delivery method may seem quaint today, but it was the first tremor of a seismic shift in how wars would be fought – truly, a 'drop' that made waves.
