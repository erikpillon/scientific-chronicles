Before the air brake, stopping a train was a precarious, manual, and often slow affair. Brakemen had to scramble across the top of moving carriages, applying brakes by hand – talk about a high-stakes job! Enter the brilliant American inventor, George Westinghouse. In 1869, he changed everything with his invention of the railway air brake. Instead of individual manual brakes, his system used compressed air to simultaneously apply brakes on all cars, significantly reducing stopping distances and making trains much, much safer. Imagine the relief! This wasn't just a comfort; it was a revolution for safety and efficiency, allowing trains to travel faster and carry more passengers and freight with confidence. Westinghouse didn't just invent a device; he engineered a fundamental shift in railway technology that saved countless lives and propelled the industrial age forward. He truly put the 'stop' in progress, and we're all safer on the rails because of it!
