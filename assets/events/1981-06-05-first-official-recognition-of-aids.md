On June 5, 1981, the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) published a report in its Morbidity and Mortality Weekly Report (MMWR) that would send ripples of fear and urgency through the global medical community. The report described five puzzling cases of Pneumocystis carinii pneumonia (PCP)—a rare infection usually only seen in severely immunocompromised patients—in young, previously healthy gay men in Los Angeles. This wasn't just a handful of unusual cases; it was the first official, albeit initially cryptic, signal of what would soon be identified as Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome (AIDS). This initial publication, quickly followed by similar reports, galvanized scientists into action, initiated an unprecedented global health crisis, and began a tragic, but ultimately transformative, scientific and social journey.
