In the mid-1980s, the AIDS epidemic was a terrifying, seemingly unstoppable force, leaving doctors and patients with little more than despair. But on March 19, 1987, a glimmer of hope arrived: the FDA fast-tracked approval for AZT (Zidovudine). Originally synthesized in 1964 by Jerome Horwitz as a potential anti-cancer drug (which it wasn't good for), AZT was repurposed and discovered to inhibit HIV replication. Its approval marked a monumental turning point, as it was the very first drug to treat AIDS. While AZT had significant side effects and wasn't a cure, it dramatically extended and improved the lives of many, transforming AIDS from an immediate death sentence into a manageable, albeit serious, condition. It proved that HIV could be fought, igniting further research that would eventually lead to today's highly effective combination therapies. This drug was a true game-changer, literally saving lives and inspiring a new era of HIV/AIDS research.
