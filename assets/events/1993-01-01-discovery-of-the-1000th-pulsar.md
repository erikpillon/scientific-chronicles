Pulsars are like the universe's most dramatic lighthouses: rapidly rotating neutron stars that emit beams of radiation, appearing to 'pulse' as they spin. The very first one was discovered by Jocelyn Bell Burnell and Antony Hewish in 1967, sparking a whole new field of astrophysics. Fast forward a few decades of intense astronomical detective work, and by the early 1990s, astronomers had cataloged their *1,000th* pulsar! This incredible milestone wasn't just a number; it represented a massive leap in our understanding of stellar evolution, extreme physics, and the bizarre objects that pepper our galaxy. It showed just how common these cosmic oddities are and gave scientists a treasure trove of data to decode the universe's most intense secrets.
