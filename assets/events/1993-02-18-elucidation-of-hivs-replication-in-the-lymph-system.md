For years after the discovery of HIV, the prevailing wisdom was that the virus lay mostly dormant in the early stages of infection, only becoming active later. But then came the mic drop from David Ho and his team in the early 1990s. Their research, notably published in 1993, proved that HIV was actively, relentlessly replicating within the lymph nodes and other lymphoid tissues right from the get-go. This wasn't just a minor detail; it was a seismic shift in understanding, revealing that the immune system was under constant, massive assault throughout the infection. This new insight fundamentally changed treatment strategies, emphasizing early and aggressive antiretroviral therapy to suppress viral replication from the outset. It was a crucial piece of the puzzle that turned HIV from a slowly ticking time bomb into a condition where early intervention could make a world of difference. It literally redefined the battle plan against AIDS.
