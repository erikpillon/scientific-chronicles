The Trans-Alaska Pipeline System (TAPS) is an extraordinary engineering saga, built to transport crude oil from the vast reserves of Alaska's North Slope to the ice-free port of Valdez. Construction began in 1974 amidst fierce environmental debates and staggering logistical challenges, including permafrost, earthquake zones, and extreme temperatures. Completed in 1977, the 800-mile pipeline, with its innovative zig-zagging design and elevated sections, became a symbol of both industrial ambition and environmental compromise. It was a monumental undertaking that forever changed Alaska's economy and ignited global discussions about energy infrastructure and ecological impact, proving that sometimes, even oil can be a scientific marvel.
