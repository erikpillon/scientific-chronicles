Before the turn of the 20th century, American physicists were a bit like lone wolves, doing groundbreaking work but without a unified pack. That changed dramatically on May 20, 1899, when 36 visionary physicists gathered at Columbia University to found the American Physical Society (APS). Their ambitious goal? To advance and disseminate the knowledge of physics, both pure and applied. Led by its first president, Arthur Gordon Webster, the APS quickly became the premier organization for physicists in the United States, hosting conferences, publishing cutting-edge research, and fostering collaboration that would propel American physics onto the global stage. From quantum mechanics to particle accelerators, the APS has been a central hub, celebrating discoveries and shaping scientific discourse for over a century – essentially, where all the cool physics kids gather.
