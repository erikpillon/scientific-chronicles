On Good Friday, March 27, 1964, Alaska was rocked by the most powerful earthquake ever recorded in North American history, a colossal magnitude 9.2 mega-thrust event. The ground shook for nearly five minutes, triggering massive tsunamis that devastated coastal communities and causing widespread land subsidence and uplift. This wasn't just a disaster; it was a geological blockbuster. Scientists, particularly George Plafker, extensively studied its effects, providing crucial evidence that bolstered the then-emerging theory of plate tectonics. It fundamentally changed how we understood seismic hazards and the dynamic nature of our planet's crust, leaving a permanent mark on both the land and scientific knowledge.
