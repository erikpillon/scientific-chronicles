In the frantic early 1980s, as AIDS cases mounted, the hunt for the causative agent was on. Enter Luc Montagnier and his team at the Pasteur Institute in France. On May 20, 1983, they announced their discovery of a new retrovirus, initially called LAV (lymphadenopathy-associated virus), isolated from a patient with AIDS. This pivotal finding, later confirmed and named Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV), finally gave researchers a target for diagnostics and treatment. While controversial claims and collaborations with Robert Gallo's U.S. team unfolded, Montagnier and Françoise Barré-Sinoussi ultimately received the Nobel Prize for their seminal work. Pinpointing the culprit was a critical first step, turning a mysterious disease into a solvable, albeit complex, scientific challenge.
