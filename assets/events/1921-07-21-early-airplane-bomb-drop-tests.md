In the years following World War I, a fiery debate raged among military strategists: could aircraft truly be a decisive weapon? Brigadier General Billy Mitchell, a fervent advocate for air power, aimed to settle the argument with a dramatic series of bomb-drop tests in 1921. Off the coast of Virginia, Mitchell's bombers attacked captured German warships, including the formidable battleship SMS Ostfriesland. The traditionalists scoffed, believing bombs couldn't possibly sink a capital ship. But on July 21, 1921, after repeated strikes, the Ostfriesland capsized and sank, proving Mitchell resoundingly right. This wasn't just a demolition derby; it was a paradigm shift. The tests demonstrated the vulnerability of even the strongest naval vessels to aerial attack, forever changing military doctrine and accelerating the development of naval aviation. Mitchell's daring challenge to conventional wisdom, backed by these explosive demonstrations, forced military leaders to rethink everything they knew about warfare, showing that the skies were now a crucial new battlefield.
