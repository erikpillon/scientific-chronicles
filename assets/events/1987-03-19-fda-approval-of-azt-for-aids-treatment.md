The 1980s were a bleak, terrifying period for those diagnosed with AIDS, with no effective treatments and a dire prognosis. Then came AZT (azidothymidine, or zidovudine). Originally synthesized in the 1960s as a potential anti-cancer drug, it was rediscovered in the mid-1980s as a potent inhibitor of HIV replication. After remarkably rapid clinical trials, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved AZT on March 19, 1987, making it the first antiretroviral drug available to treat AIDS. While not a cure and accompanied by significant side effects, AZT was a monumental breakthrough. It proved that HIV could be targeted and that intervention could prolong lives. This moment sparked hope, accelerated further research, and paved the way for the powerful combination therapies that have since transformed HIV from a death sentence into a manageable chronic condition.
