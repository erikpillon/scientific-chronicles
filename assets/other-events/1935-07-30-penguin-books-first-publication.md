The paperback revolution began with the publication of ten Penguin books, inspired by Allen Lane's frustration with the lack of cheap, good fiction. The original colour-coded books, costing six pence, included works by Ernest Hemingway, Dorothy L. Sayers, and Agatha Christie. The concept was an instant success, leading to Penguin becoming a standalone publisher in 1936 and selling three million paperbacks within a year.
