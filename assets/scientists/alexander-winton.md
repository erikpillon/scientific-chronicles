Move over, Henry Ford! Before the Model T, there was Alexander Winton, a Scottish-born mechanical whiz who emigrated to the U.S. and became one of America's first major automobile manufacturers. Winton wasn't just building cars; he was practically inventing the industry as he went along. In 1897, his Winton Motor Carriage Company sold its first vehicle, making it the first American company to sell a motorcar. He even famously drove one of his own cars from Cleveland to New York in 1897 and again in 1899, in the earliest recorded cross-country drives, to prove their reliability (and perhaps just for a bit of a laugh!). He also developed advanced features like the first steering wheel (replacing tillers) and four-wheel brakes. Though he later lost market share to mass production, Winton's initial spark helped ignite the American automotive revolution. He showed everyone that horseless carriages weren't just a novelty; they were the future.
