Before Audubon swooped in, there was Alexander Wilson, the unsung "Father of American Ornithology." Born in Scotland in 1766, Wilson was a weaver turned poet, turned schoolteacher, before finding his true calling among the birds of North America. He embarked on epic journeys across the young United States, often on foot, driven by an almost obsessive desire to observe and illustrate every bird he encountered. His monumental nine-volume work, *American Ornithology*, published between 1808 and 1814, was a groundbreaking achievement, detailing hundreds of species with stunning illustrations (many by his own hand). Sadly, he died at 47, exhausted by his incredible endeavors, just as his masterpiece was nearing completion. His dedication laid the essential groundwork for future naturalists, proving that you don't need a fancy lab to make monumental scientific contributions – just a pair of sturdy boots and an insatiable curiosity for the natural world.
