Charles Goodyear was an absolute madman for rubber. In the 1830s, natural rubber was pretty useless—it froze solid in the winter and melted into a sticky puddle in the summer. Goodyear was convinced he could fix it. 

He was so obsessed that he bankrupted his family, sold his children’s schoolbooks for experiment money, and ended up in debtor's prison multiple times. He even walked around wearing entirely rubber outfits that smelled terrible. Then, one day in 1839, he accidentally dropped a mixture of rubber, sulfur, and lead onto a red-hot stove. Instead of melting, it charred and turned into a tough, elastic, weatherproof material. He had just discovered vulcanization. 

Despite inventing the material that basically runs the modern world, he was terrible at business and died deeply in debt. Today, his name is plastered on blimps, tires, and massive research facilities worldwide (including that huge Goodyear innovation center right in Luxembourg!). He didn't get the cash, but he definitely got the legacy. 🚗🔥
