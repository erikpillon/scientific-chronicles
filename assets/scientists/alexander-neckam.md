Way back in the 12th century, when most folks were worried about crusades and crop yields, Alexander Neckam was busy being a medieval genius. This English scholar, abbot, and all-around polymath penned books on everything from grammar to natural science. But here's his mic drop moment: he wrote the earliest known European description of the magnetic compass in his treatise "De naturis rerum" (On the Natures of Things). He explained how a magnetized needle, floating on water, could point sailors north, a discovery that was truly revolutionary for navigation. Neckam proved that even in the Middle Ages, some minds were already pointing the way to a more scientifically enlightened future.
