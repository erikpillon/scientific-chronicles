A.R. Radcliffe-Brown was the architect of structural functionalism, essentially asking: 'How do societies keep from falling apart?' He viewed societies like living organisms, where every institution – from family structures to rituals – had a function in maintaining social order and stability. He wasn't big on speculating about origins (unlike some of his contemporaries); he just wanted to know how societies *worked* right now. His fieldwork in the Andaman Islands and Australia provided rich data for his theories, making anthropology less about exotic tales and more about rigorous, comparative analysis. Imagine a social scientist who sees society as a complex machine, dissecting each part to understand its contribution to the whole. His influence was massive, shaping how generations of anthropologists studied social structures and their functions, even if later critics found his approach a bit too static.
