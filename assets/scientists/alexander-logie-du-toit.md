Before plate tectonics was a thing, there was Alexander Logie Du Toit, a South African geologist with an eye for ancient connections. While many scoffed at Alfred Wegener's wild idea of continents drifting apart, Du Toit saw the evidence staring him in the face – or rather, spread across the Southern Hemisphere. He meticulously compared rock formations, fossils, and glacial deposits between South America, Africa, Australia, and India, gathering concrete proof that these landmasses were once snuggled up together in a supercontinent he dubbed "Gondwana." His book, "Our Wandering Continents," wasn't just a nod to Wegener; it was a powerful, data-rich argument that helped pave the way for a geological revolution, proving that some scientists know how to stick to their (continental) guns.
