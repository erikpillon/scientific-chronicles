Alexander Ross Clarke wasn't content with just knowing the Earth was round; he wanted to know *exactly* how round (or not-so-round) it was. This British geodesist was obsessed with measuring the planet's precise shape and size, a monumental task in the 19th century. Through meticulous triangulation and astronomical observations, he crunched vast amounts of data to develop what became known as the Clarke Spheroid of 1866 and 1880. These mathematical models of the Earth's ellipsoid were so accurate they became international standards, influencing cartography and navigation for decades. Clarke literally helped us get a better grip on the planet, proving that sometimes, even the most enormous problems can be solved with careful measurement and a lot of math.
