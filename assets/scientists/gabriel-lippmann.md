We have another Luxembourg-born genius on the board. Back in the late 1800s, photographs were strictly black and white. A few people tried to make color photos by just painting over the black-and-white prints or using messy chemical dyes. 

Gabriel Lippmann thought that was incredibly sloppy. He was a physicist, so he decided to solve the problem from first principles. 

Instead of using dyes, he used the physical properties of light itself. By placing a mirror at the back of a photographic plate, he forced incoming light waves to bounce back and crash into themselves. This created "interference patterns" right inside the film emulsion. When you looked at the developed plate, it accurately bent white light to recreate the exact colors of the original scene. It was pure, brilliant physics. It was terribly complicated to mass-produce, but it proved color photography was a hard science, and it snagged him the 1908 Nobel Prize. 📸🌈
