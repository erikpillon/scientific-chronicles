When epidemics struck, Alexander Langmuir was the guy you wanted on your team. This American epidemiologist, brother of Nobel laureate Irving Langmuir, practically wrote the book on how to track and tackle disease outbreaks. Joining the newly formed Communicable Disease Center (now CDC) in the 1940s, he had a genius idea: train smart, quick-thinking doctors to be "disease detectives." He founded the Epidemic Intelligence Service (EIS), transforming public health from a reactive mess to a proactive science. From smallpox eradication to understanding the flu, Langmuir’s work armed the world with the tools to fight invisible enemies, saving countless lives and proving that sometimes, the best defense is a really good offense.
