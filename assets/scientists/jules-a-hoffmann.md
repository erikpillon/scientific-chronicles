Before Jules Hoffmann came along, scientists knew we had an immune system, but the actual "first responder" part of it was a massive mystery. Hoffmann decided to figure it out by studying something incredibly glamorous: infected fruit flies. 

In the 1990s, he discovered that these tiny flies had specific receptors (called Toll receptors) that instantly recognized nasty bacteria and fungi, triggering a rapid defense. It turns out, human biology runs on the exact same ancient code. We have Toll-like receptors that act as the front-line security guards for our bodies. If a virus breaks in, these receptors sound the alarm before the rest of your immune system even gets out of bed. 

His fruit fly obsession fundamentally changed modern medicine, completely rewrote our understanding of innate immunity, and casually earned him a Nobel Prize in 2011. Not bad for a guy staring at bugs in a lab. 🪰🔬
