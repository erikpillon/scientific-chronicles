Albert Hoyt Taylor was an American physicist who basically gave the world eyes in the dark – or rather, in the air and sea. Working for the Naval Research Laboratory, he and his team were dabbling with radio waves when they noticed something weird: ships passing by interfered with their signals. Instead of shrugging it off, Taylor realized this interference could be used to *detect* objects. This seemingly simple observation in 1922 was the primordial soup from which radar technology emerged. He wasn't just a theorist; his practical experiments laid the essential groundwork for what would become a game-changing technology for navigation, weather forecasting, and, famously, military defense during WWII. So, next time a plane gets safely to its destination, tip your hat to Taylor, who taught us how to bounce radio waves off things and see the unseen.
