Imagine a world where electricity flows with zero resistance, zipping along without losing any energy – that's the dream of superconductivity! For decades, this magic only happened at super-chilly, impractical temperatures. Then came Karl Alexander Müller, a Swiss physicist with a knack for materials. In 1986, working with Johannes Georg Bednorz at IBM, he made a jaw-dropping discovery: a ceramic material that became superconducting at a much, much higher temperature than previously thought possible. This wasn't just a minor tweak; it opened up entirely new avenues for research and applications, from incredibly efficient power grids to levitating trains. Their breakthrough earned them the Nobel Prize in Physics just a year later, a lightning-fast recognition of its monumental impact. Müller proved that sometimes, the biggest breakthroughs aren't found by looking harder in the old places, but by daring to explore entirely new materials and seeing what unexpected wonders they might hold.
