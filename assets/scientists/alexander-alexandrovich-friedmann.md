Before we had the Big Bang, we had Alexander Friedmann quietly doing the math that made it all possible. This Russian and Soviet physicist and mathematician, way back in the 1920s, took Einstein's general relativity equations and ran with them – not just content with a static universe, as Einstein initially preferred. Friedmann dared to imagine a dynamic universe, one that could expand or contract. His "Friedmann equations" described various cosmic scenarios, including one where the universe started from an incredibly dense state and expanded outwards. He essentially laid the mathematical groundwork for the expanding universe, years before Edwin Hubble's observational evidence solidified the idea. Tragically, he died young from typhoid, never fully seeing his theoretical insights confirmed. But his equations remain the bedrock of modern cosmology, a testament to a mind that could see the universe's grand story unfolding in abstract symbols. He literally wrote the universe's future.
