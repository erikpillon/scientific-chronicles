How do you figure out what the Sun is made of without actually going there? You ask Anders Ångström. 

This 19th-century Swedish physicist was absolutely obsessed with light. He took the light coming from the Sun, passed it through a prism, and studied the exact barcode of colors and missing lines it produced. By doing this, he successfully proved that the Sun contains hydrogen—a massive leap forward in astrophysics. 

Because he was measuring light waves, he had to deal with ridiculously small numbers. He defined a unit of length equal to one ten-billionth of a meter. The scientific community respected his work so much they permanently named that microscopic measurement after him: the ångström (Å). When your name becomes the official vocabulary for atomic-level physics, you know you've made it. ☀️📏
